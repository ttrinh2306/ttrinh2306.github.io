Investigating the foraging efficiency of ants when infected with Ophiocordyceps (M.S. thesis & publication)


Numerous parasites have developed techniques to manipulate host behaviors to enhance their own transmission. A evident example of this is seen in how Ophiocordyceps influences carpenter ants. These ants, under the fungus's control, climb plants and lock their jaws in a fixed 'death-grip.' After killing the ant, the fungus grows a stalk to disperse infectious spores. While much research has focused on this final control stage, earlier subtle behavioral alterations in ants before biting have been less studied.

I conducted experiments to see if early Ophiocordyceps infection impacts Camponotus floridanus ants' foraging behaviors. I analyzed their foraging paths in a maze and compared infected ants with healthy ones and those infected with Beauveria bassiana, a fungus that does not manipulate host behavior. The analyses I used included survival analyses, zero-one beta models (package: gamlss), and times series analyses.

Our findings show that Ophiocordyceps-infected ants displayed irregular activity patterns, were less involved in successful foraging, and seemed less capable of communicating with other ants. We believe these behavioral shifts aid the fungus's spread by reducing the likelihood of aggressive responses from other ants. Interestingly, while Beauveria-infected ants maintained regular patterns, they too showed reduced foraging efficiency, indicating these behavioral changes might partly be general effects of infection.

This research sheds light on the strategies parasites use to control hosts, the dynamics of other parasite-host interactions, and broadly on the behavioral ecology of infectious diseases.

Remediating missing geospatial and collection metadata from genomic datasets in the International Nucleotide Sequence Database Collaboration (INDSC)


I collaborated with 12 other graduate students and 12 professional researchers from four countries in a distributed datathon. Our goal was to assess the availability of critical missing metadata tied to BioProjects in the INSCD and test the theory that metadata availability diminishes over time. My specific role involved curating and organizing geospatial and collection metadata by sourcing it from published papers, online repositories, and direct author communication. This effort significantly contributed to the creation of the Genomic Observatories Meta-Database (GEOME).
