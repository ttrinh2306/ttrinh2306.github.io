Developing an app that matches users with a CBS Survivor player using LLM


For those who don’t know me, I am a HUGE CBS Survivor Fan - I’d love to play one day! I think it’s the most incredible game that tests athletic finesse, social strategy, and mental will power. Entering this project, I wanted to incorporate data that genuinely interested me, deepen my understand of Large Language Models (LLMs), and learn how to build a full-stack app from scratch to deployment.

In this project, I developed an app that identifies which Survivor contestant you most resemble. However, instead of using a standard multiple-choice questionnaire, it poses a series of short-answer questions about your background, interests, etc. The app employs an LLM, specifically BERT, to analyze your responses and compare them with biographies of all the players who have ever participated in the game, determining your closest match.

Specifically, I web scraped Survivor biographies (Python package: BeautifulSoup). I then constructed the back-end, utilizing the BERT LLM model to tokenize and compare biographies (Python packages: transformers, tensor flow, sklearn). From there, I developed a user-facing React app and used a Flask API to integrate the back-end with the front end (Python package: flask). Finally, I deployed the model on AWS EC2.

Investigating the foraging efficiency of ants when infected with Ophiocordyceps (M.S. thesis & publication)


Numerous parasites have developed techniques to manipulate host behaviors to enhance their own transmission. A evident example of this is seen in how Ophiocordyceps influences carpenter ants. These ants, under the fungus's control, climb plants and lock their jaws in a fixed 'death-grip.' After killing the ant, the fungus grows a stalk to disperse infectious spores. While much research has focused on this final control stage, earlier subtle behavioral alterations in ants before biting have been less studied. For this work, I investigated if the foraging behaviour of Camponotus floridanus ants is notably affected during early stage Ophiocordyceps infection. This research sheds light on the strategies parasites use to control hosts, the dynamics of other parasite-host interactions, and broadly on the behavioral ecology of infectious diseases.

For this project, I assumed responsibility for designing, researching, conducting experiments, and implementing machine-learning solutions from prototype to publication. Leveraging my proficiency in R, I conducted exploratory analysis, visualization, and implemented predictive models (i.e., generalized linear mixed models, zero-one-inflated beta models, linear regressions, survival analyses, time series analyses). This work culminated into a first-author peer-reviewed journal publication.
